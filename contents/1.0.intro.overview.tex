\label{chap:introduction} In the last decade, commercial resistive
random-access memory (ReRAM) has entered mass production in the form of
embedded non-volatile data storage modules for low-power
microcontrollers and edge devices. These devices introduce stochastic
timing properties with no direct analog in conventional memory
technologies. The architectural features that produce these properties
are not publicly disclosed by manufacturers. This combination of novel
timing behavior and opaque architecture creates an underexplored attack
surface with implications for both the security of deployed systems and
the integrity of security primitives built on top of them.

This dissertation proposes novel timing side-channels in commercial
ReRAM through systematic experimental characterization of write latency
in deployed devices. It identifies the dominant structural factors that
impact timing behavior, demonstrates that those factors are recoverable
through non-invasive side-channel analysis, and applies that structural
knowledge to the characterization of stochastic timing behavior in
commercial products.

The contributions of this dissertation span two coupled problems: the
recovery of \emph{hidden} device architecture through timing analysis,
and the characterization of timing-based entropy sources in the context
of these architecturual details. The solution to these two problems
captures data-dependent switching dynamics that are required to reason
about the full timing side-channel attack surface of commercial ReRAM.
The remainder of this chapter motivates this work in the context of
commercial ReRAM deployment and timing side-channel security
(\S\ref{sec:motivation}), states the research objectives and
contributions of this dissertation w.r.t. gaps in the existing
literature (\S\ref{sec:objectives}), and describes the organization of
the remaining chapters (\S\ref{sec:organization}).
